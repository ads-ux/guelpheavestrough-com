Planning an Eavestrough Installation in Guelph

Seamless aluminum is the standard choice for Guelph homes for good reason. Unlike sectional eavestrough sold in fixed lengths, seamless systems are fabricated on-site from a roll of aluminum stock, cut to the exact length of each wall run. The only joints are at corners and downspout connections, which removes the majority of the leak points that plague older sectional installations as their caulking dries and shifts through freeze-thaw cycles.

Getting the size right matters. A 4-inch profile was the old standard, but most Guelph homes today are better served by 5-inch or 6-inch K-style eavestrough, especially with heavier rainfall events becoming more common. Sizing depends on roof area, pitch, and valley concentration — see our eavestrough sizing guide for how we work this out for a given roof.

Fascia condition drives cost more than anything else. If the board behind your existing eavestrough is rotted, it has to be replaced before new eavestrough can be properly fastened — skipping this step is the single most common reason a "cheap" installation fails within a few years. We inspect and flag fascia condition at every quote.

Downspout placement is easy to get wrong. A downspout that discharges too close to the foundation, or into a clogged underground drain, defeats the purpose of the whole system. We map the full drainage path — not just the trough itself — before finalizing a quote.

New Install or Repair? How to Tell

An isolated problem — a leaking joint, one sagging section, a clogged downspout — is almost always a repair. Full replacement makes more sense when the system is over 20 years old, showing rust or corrosion across multiple sections, undersized for your current roof, or failing in more than one location at once.
